Epidemic Spreading

Mathematical Modeling
Three epidemic models (SIS, SIR, SAIR) with infection rate β, curing rate α, and spreading rate τ = α/β.
→
03
📊
Monte Carlo
Thousands of stochastic simulations produce reliable statistical predictions of infection percentages over time.
→
04
🔍
Source Detection
Jordan Center and Distance Centrality algorithms attempt to identify the original Patient Zero from a network snapshot.
SIS
Susceptible → Infected → Susceptible
Diseases you can catch again — flu, cold
S—β→I—α→S

Infected nodes spread to susceptible neighbors with probability β. Cured individuals become susceptible again — epidemic can plateau indefinitely if τ = α/β is high enough.

SIR
Susceptible → Infected → Recovered
Diseases granting immunity — COVID-19
S—β→I—α→R

Recovered individuals gain permanent immunity. The infected curve always rises then falls back to zero. The epidemic always ends.

SAIR
Susceptible → Alert → Infected → Recovered
Most realistic — models human behavioral response
S—κ→A—β₁→I—α→R

Individuals surrounded by infected neighbors enter an Alert state (masks, hygiene) with rate κ, reducing infection rate to β₁ < β₀. Models real human behavioral adaptation.

Given a snapshot of who is infected or recovered — can we mathematically trace back to the origin? Two algorithms compared:

🎯
Jordan Center

Finds the node minimizing the maximum shortest path to any infected/recovered node. Uses dijkstra_shortest_paths — purely spatial reasoning.

📏
Distance Centrality

Minimizes a weighted sum of distances to all infected/recovered nodes. Recovered nodes weighted more heavily (×α) since they were infected earlier.
