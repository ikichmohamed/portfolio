Graph Theory Project · ENSEEIHT SN · Duo
Epidemic Spreading
Graph-based mathematical modeling of COVID-19 epidemic propagation through human contact networks.
3
Models
1000+
Node Network
🎓 ENSEEIHT
01
🌐
Contact Network
The population is modeled as a graph where each node = person and each edge = contact. Disease only travels through edges — capturing real-world social structures like families, workplaces, and neighborhoods.
→
02
🧮
Mathematical Modeling
Three epidemic models (SIS, SIR, SAIR) simulate disease spread using infection rate β, curing rate α, and effective spreading rate τ = α/β across 3 real-world-inspired networks.
→
03
📊
Monte Carlo Simulation
Since each spread is stochastic, the models run thousands of simulations to produce reliable statistical predictions of infection percentages over time for each disease configuration.
→
04
🔍
Source Detection
Given a snapshot of who is infected/recovered at a point in time, two graph algorithms (Jordan Center and Distance Centrality) attempt to trace back and identify the original Patient Zero.
Contact Networks Used
🥋
Karate Club (karat7)
34 nodes — inspired by Zachary's famous karate club problem. Dense small social network.
🎓
N7 Students (n7_2A)
220 nodes across 3 departments (SN, MF2E, 3EA). Dense intra-dept, sparse inter-dept connections.
🏙️
Toulouse Neighborhood
1000 nodes generated via Barabási-Albert model — mimics real-world scale-free social networks.
SIS
Susceptible → Infected → Susceptible
For diseases you can catch again — flu, cold
S —β→ I —α→ S

Each infected node spreads disease to susceptible neighbors with probability β per time step. Each infected node recovers with probability α. Cured individuals become susceptible again — the epidemic can plateau indefinitely if τ = α/β is high enough.

β infection rate α curing rate τ = α/β spreading rate
SIR
Susceptible → Infected → Recovered
For diseases that grant immunity — COVID-19
S —β→ I —α→ R

Like SIS but recovered individuals gain permanent immunity — they cannot be reinfected. This causes the infected curve to always rise then fall back to zero, unlike SIS which can maintain a steady infected population. The epidemic always ends.

β infection rate α curing rate Recovered = immune
SAIR
Susceptible → Alert → Infected → Recovered
Most realistic — models human behavioral response
S —κ→ A —β₁→ I —α→ R

Individuals observe their neighbors — if surrounded by infected people they enter an Alert state (wear masks, wash hands) with rate κ. Alerted individuals have a reduced infection rate β₁ < β₀. This models real human behavioral adaptation and significantly slows epidemic peaks.

β₀ normal infection rate β₁ alert infection rate κ alerting rate α curing rate

Given only a snapshot of who is currently infected or recovered in the network — can we mathematically trace back to who started the epidemic? Two graph algorithms were implemented and compared:

🎯
Jordan Center

Finds the node that minimizes the maximum shortest path to any infected or recovered node. The logic: Patient Zero is likely at the geometric center of the infection cluster.

argminv∈C maxu∈C d(u,v)
  • Uses dijkstra_shortest_paths from LightGraphs
  • Works on the subgraph of infected + recovered nodes
  • Simple and fast — purely spatial reasoning
📏
Distance Centrality

Finds the node that minimizes a weighted sum of distances to all infected and recovered nodes. Recovered nodes are weighted more heavily (×α) since they likely got sick earlier — closer to the origin.

argminv Σ [𝟙I(n)·d(v,n) + α·𝟙R(n)·d(v,n)]
  • Weighs recovered nodes more — they were infected earlier
  • More nuanced than Jordan — accounts for epidemic timeline
  • Tested on karat7 and a 50×50 grid network
💡
Key Insight
The stochastic nature of infection makes this problem inherently hard — different initial conditions can lead to identical snapshots. These spatial heuristics approximate the source but cannot guarantee correctness. The denser and more symmetric the network, the harder Patient Zero identification becomes.
